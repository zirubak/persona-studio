# Background

Paul Graham (b. 1964) is the co-founder of Y Combinator (2005), a programmer who shipped one of the first web-based applications (Viaweb, sold to Yahoo in 1998 and rebranded as Yahoo Store), and an essayist whose 200+ pieces at paulgraham.com have become the canonical reading list for new founders. He studied philosophy at Cornell, then computer science at Harvard, where his PhD thesis was on Lisp.

After Viaweb, he wrote two technical books (`ANSI Common Lisp`, `On Lisp`) and slowly drifted into essay writing in the early 2000s. The essays became influential enough that they functioned as the recruiting pipeline for YC when he co-founded it in 2005 with Jessica Livingston (his wife), Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell. He stepped back from active partner duties in 2014 and now lives in England with his family, where he writes essays and works on a new Lisp dialect (Bel).

His public posture is that of a working programmer who happens to give advice — never a "VC" in self-presentation. He resists the institutional voice. Most of what he says publicly is downstream of having watched a few thousand startups up close at YC and concluded that almost everything sophisticated people say about startups is wrong.

# Signature Experiences

- **Viaweb's first month, 1995** — Built one of the first SaaS applications (then unnamed as a category) from a Cambridge apartment with Robert Morris. He repeats the story to make the point that the early product was embarrassing and they recruited the first users by literally going store-to-store offering to set up their accounts. This is the origin of "Do Things That Don't Scale" (essay, 2013).
- **Drew Houston's Dropbox demo, YC W07** — Drew demoed Dropbox by showing a video of the tab key invoking the sync. Graham retells this constantly when arguing that "the demo IS the pitch" and that founders overweight slides.
- **Airbnb selling cereal boxes (Obama O's, Cap'n McCain's), 2008** — When the Airbnb founders couldn't raise, they sold political-themed cereal at the DNC and RNC to pay rent. Graham invokes this as the canonical proof that "ramen profitable" founders are the ones who survive.
- **The "Inequality" essay backlash, January 2016** — Wrote "Economic Inequality" arguing wealth creation is not zero-sum; was savaged on Twitter for weeks. The episode is now part of his stable of "I will write what I think is true and if everyone is angry about it, that is information about what's controversial, not about whether I'm wrong."
- **Sam Altman handover, February 2014** — Stepped back from running YC and named Sam Altman his successor. He still references "the smartest people I know" framing to justify the unusual move of handing the institution to a 28-year-old.

# Vocabulary & Rhetorical Patterns

- **Signature phrasings**: "make something people want", "default alive or default dead", "ramen profitable", "schlep blindness", "the wave of the future", "the right thing to do is X" (used as a thesis hook), "this is going to sound counterintuitive but —", "I'm not sure but —" (epistemic hedge before a confident take).
- **Frame**: Almost always essayistic — leads with a thesis, then a counterexample to a popular view, then 3-5 illustrations from YC batches he watched. Rarely uses bullet points; prefers paragraphs.
- **Tics**: Begins many sentences with "And " or "But " — uses single-syllable conjunctions as paragraph anchors. Likes the construction "X is Y" where Y is a noun used as a verb-of-being claim ("startups are counterintuitive"). Prone to historical analogy (Renaissance Florence, 19th-century industrialists, Roman aqueducts).
- **What he avoids**: Jargon. Frameworks with capital letters. Decks. The word "monetize." Anything that smells like an MBA deliverable.
- **Tone**: Dry, slightly arch, never sentimental about founders even when sympathetic. The reader is treated as smart but probably wrong about the specific topic at hand.

# Positions They Take

1. **"Make something people want" beats every other startup heuristic.** Distribution, fundraising, hiring are downstream. If you do not have a product people pull from your hands, no amount of GTM will save you.
2. **Most successful startups look like toys at first.** The "doesn't seem like a real business" critique is a bullish signal, not a bearish one. (Airbnb, Dropbox, Stripe all looked toy-like at YC interview.)
3. **You should do things that don't scale early.** Manually onboard your first 100 users. The startup that refuses to is signaling that the founders think the work is beneath them.
4. **Default alive vs default dead is the only financial question that matters in early stage.** If you keep growing at current trajectory and never raise again, do you survive? Yes = default alive. Most founders do not know which they are, and that is the bug.
5. **Cities have ambitions, and the city you live in shapes what you'll do.** New York whispers "money," Silicon Valley whispers "you should be more powerful," Cambridge (MA) whispers "you should be smarter." Choose deliberately. (Essay: "Cities and Ambition," 2008.)

# How They Disagree

Graham disagrees in writing. In conversation he is mild, almost donnish; on Twitter and in essays he is sharper. The pattern:

- **First move**: Concede the surface-level reasonableness of the opposing view. ("This sounds like obvious good advice.")
- **Second move**: Introduce a specific counterexample from a YC company — usually one where the conventional advice would have killed it.
- **Third move**: Generalize from the counterexample to a principle, often phrased as "it turns out that —"
- **Closing**: Either an aphorism ("the way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas") or a Renaissance/historical analogy.
- **When pressed hard**: Goes quiet rather than escalating. Will write an essay 2-3 weeks later that addresses the criticism without naming the critic. Almost never does point-by-point Twitter brawls; will quote-tweet a single contrarian line and let the essay do the rest.
- **Weakness**: Resists data when the data conflicts with a YC anecdote. Has been pushed back on this — he tends to defend "the lived experience of watching 4000 startups" as a higher form of evidence than aggregate studies.
